## 1. The decision this informs

This dossier informs **Inflection Point 3: vertical-first vs. horizontal-first GTM** for the Agent Notary Layer (Products A + B). The question is whether Clockchain leads with Legal — eDiscovery & Contract Automation as a beachhead vertical, or sells a horizontal "agent receipt" primitive across all verticals at once.

The evidence here argues for **vertical-first, with Legal as the lead beachhead**, for four reasons that are specific to this vertical and hard to replicate elsewhere:

1. **A named, dated regulatory forcing function exists** (FRE 902(13)/(14) today; proposed Rule 707 and EU AI Act Art. 12–13 on the horizon) — most verticals have only generalized "AI governance" pressure.
2. **A concrete, accepted legal architecture already works in court** — Page Vault's "qualified person" affidavit model under FRE 902(13) — which Clockchain's B5 Receipt Minting can mirror, substituting a cryptographic notary for the vendor-CTO affidavit. This is a rare case where the legal precedent for the *shape* of the product already exists.
3. **The integration surface is unusually concrete and recent** — Anthropic's May 2026 "Claude for Legal" shipped 20+ MCP connectors, making MCP the lingua franca of the legal-AI stack and giving Clockchain a single, low-friction hook point.
4. **A pull channel sits outside the legal-ops budget** — legal malpractice insurers are independently demanding timestamped AI usage logs at renewal (see §5; confidence caveat noted there).

The counter-argument for horizontal-first is real: **no US rule names "autonomous agent actions" as a distinct legal category** — every applicable rule was written for human actors or generic electronic systems and is being stretched by analogy. That cuts both ways. It means there is no mandated standard to comply with (slows procurement), but it also means **Clockchain can define the de facto standard before any rule mandates one** (first-mover advantage). The recommendation: lead vertical in Legal to win the reference architecture and the FRE-902 framing, then generalize horizontally. The FINRA template (see §2) is the bridge — it is the clearest published spec of what an agent log must contain and will likely be cited into legal contexts.

## 2. The evidentiary standard

The core synthesized requirement: **a court-admissible record of an automated agent action must contain eight elements** — (1) agent identity (system, model version, deployment instance); (2) delegating authority (which human/institution, under what scope); (3) cryptographically verifiable timestamp with provenance chain; (4) inputs (prompts, documents accessed, tool calls); (5) intermediate decision pathway, not just output; (6) outputs; (7) tamper-evident integrity proof (hash chain); (8) human review event. **Rules 707 and 37(e) together mean the absence of items 1–7 creates inadmissibility risk and spoliation-sanctions risk simultaneously.**

| Rule / standard | Status & key dates | Penalty / consequence | Reaches autonomous agents? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **FRE 902(13)/(14)** | In force since **Dec 1, 2017** (confirmed) | Establishes *authenticity* only — needs a separate hearsay hook (usually 803(6)) | Reaches "records generated by an electronic process or system"; courts interpret this to include automated pipelines. **No published case has yet applied 902(13) to an autonomous-agent record** (confirmed gap) |
| **Proposed FRE Rule 707** (machine-generated evidence) | Draft **Aug 16, 2025**; comment closed **Feb 16, 2026**; committee final vote **May 7, 2026**; earliest effective **Dec 1, 2027** (dates confirmed) | Requires Rule 702 expert-testimony standard for AI-generated evidence | Applies *only when proponent acknowledges AI generation*; does **not** distinguish autonomous agents from human-directed AI; does **not** cover authenticity disputes |
| **FRCP 37(e)** spoliation | Operative since **2015**; *Hoffer v. Tellone* (2d Cir., **Feb 13, 2025**, confirmed) | Severe 37(e)(2) sanctions require **specific intent to deprive**, not negligence | AI conversation/action logs are ESI. **No litigation-hold exemption.** SDNY (Mag. J. Wang, **May 13, 2025**, NYT v. OpenAI) ordered OpenAI to halt automated deletion of output logs (confirmed) |
| **FRCP 26(b)(1)/(f)** proportionality + meet-and-confer | Operative | Absent AI-review audit trail is non-defensible per Sedona/court practice | Direct workflow demand for agent-action logs at the eDiscovery layer |
| **ABA Formal Op. 512** | Issued **Jul 29, 2024** (confirmed) | Rule 1.1 competence + Rule 5.3 supervisory duty extend to AI tools; AI work product must be attorney-reviewed | Places AI tools in the same category as nonlawyer supervision |
| **EU AI Act Art. 12–13** | Enforcement **Aug 2, 2026** (confirmed); possible Digital Omnibus delay to **Dec 2027** (unfinalized) | Up to **EUR 15M or 3% global turnover**; **6-month minimum** log retention (confirmed) | High-risk AI must auto-log timestamped inputs/outputs/parameters for full lifetime; legal review at scale likely in scope |
| **FINRA 2026 Oversight Report** | Published **Dec 2025** (confirmed exists, addresses agentic AI) | Books-and-records obligations | **See refutation below** — the specific rule citations are a secondary gloss, not FINRA's verbatim language |

**Two refutations that materially change how we cite this vertical:**

- **FINRA framing is overstated (REFUTED).** The dossier presented "outputs alone are insufficient; firms must retain… intermediate tool calls, data retrievals, decision pathways" and the rule stack (4511, 17a-4, 3110/3120) **as if it were FINRA's own language.** The adversarial verdict confirms this is the **Snell & Wilmer law firm's interpretive gloss**, not verbatim FINRA text. FINRA's GenAI section mentions Rule 3110 once, generically, and does not quote the "intermediate tool calls" requirement. **What survives:** FINRA *does* flag auditability and transparency for AI agents, and it remains the clearest *published regulatory signal* of what an agent log should contain in a regulated industry. **Cite it as a directional signal and an interpretive consensus, never as a mandatory recordkeeping spec.**

- **ABA Op. 512 details are over-claimed (REFUTED on three points).** Drop these: that 47 state bars have "adopted" Op. 512 (category error — states adopt Model Rules, not specific opinions; unverifiable); that the opinion requires reviews to be "**logged**" (interpolation, not text); and that it requires a partner to "**reconstruct what the AI did**" (the phrase is not in the opinion). **What survives and is confirmed:** Op. 512 extends Rule 5.3 supervisory obligations to AI tools and requires attorney review of AI work product. The *demand* for agent-identity records is a defensible inference from supervisory logic — but present it as **our analytical inference, not as a quoted ABA requirement.**

**The genuine whitespace (confirmed by the verdict):** FRE 902(13)/(14) has existed since 2017, but **no published case has applied it to an autonomous-agent record**, and the exact "qualified person" certification chain for a multi-model agentic system has never been litigated. Proposed Rule 707 — the most obviously relevant new rule — **explicitly does not apply when authenticity is disputed.** So Rule 707 helps with "we used AI and want to admit the output" but does nothing for "*did this AI actually do this action?*" That authentication gap (902(13) for agent records) is **uncovered by the proposed rule and unoccupied by any competitor.**

## 3. Competitive landscape

The field splits into three non-overlapping categories, and **no player has a decentralized, BFT-validator-backed, multi-source-UTC, court-admissible audit chain for AI agent actions.** The five-primitive combination (BFT quorum + 5-layer audit chain + dedicated subnets + multi-source UTC + token-staked slashing) is genuinely unoccupied.

**Key competitive reads:**
- **Page Vault's qualified-person model is the precedent, not the threat.** It proves the FRE 902(13) affidavit architecture works in court for web evidence. **No one has applied it to AI agent action receipts.** Clockchain's cryptographic notary substitutes for the vendor-CTO affidavit — that is the B5 thesis in one sentence.
- **Truescreen and Clockchain are orthogonal but could collide at the MCP hook.** Truescreen certifies a *digital artifact*; Clockchain certifies an *agent's action sequence* (who, what authority, what input, when, what decision path). They are complementary on paper — but a buyer who wants a single notary layer will not buy both. Clockchain's differentiation is BFT quorum + multi-source UTC + the A1–A6 identity chain, none of which Truescreen has.

## 4. Agent density & trajectory

**Density is high and accelerating.** At least 7–8 distinct agentic legal-AI products launched or materially upgraded in 2025–26: Harvey Agents + Agent Builder, CoCounsel Legal (Thomson Reuters, Aug 2025), Luminance Autonomous Negotiation, DISCO Cecilia Q&A (Feb 9, 2026), Relativity aiR (Review/Privilege/Case Strategy), Ironclad Agentic Autopilot, Legora Agents, Hebbia. Every major legal-tech stack now ships an "agent" layer.

**Adoption trajectory (confirmed figures):**
- Legal AI adoption among professionals: **31% (2025) → 69% (2026)** (8am Legal Industry Report).
- **87% of general counsel** now use generative AI, up from 44% a year prior — in-house is outrunning firms.
- eDiscovery-specific: **37% actively using AI** (mid-2025) vs. 12% two years prior (Everlaw/Lighthouse).
- Harvey: **400,000 daily** *agentic* queries (note: Harvey's own term is "agentic queries," narrower than "queries"); valuation $5B → $11B in eight months (the $11B raise was *reported* Feb 9, 2026 and *formally announced* Mar 25, 2026).
- DISCO Cecilia: >300% growth — **but the metric is "customer databases leveraging the Cecilia platform," not Q&A adoption specifically** (verdict gap; do not collapse).

**True autonomy is narrowly scoped — human-in-the-loop remains the rule:**

| Tier | Description | Examples | Production status 2026 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Fully autonomous** | AI executes end-to-end, zero human | **Luminance** agent-to-agent NDA negotiation (Trench Group: **80% of contracts** handled without legal intervention; review time 150→30 min, confirmed) | Production, but **only low-risk contract categories** |
| **2 — Supervised-autonomous** | AI executes steps; human approves at checkpoints | Harvey Agents (due diligence, contract analysis); CoCounsel Deep Research; DISCO Cecilia Q&A; Ironclad Redlining | **Most production deployments live here** |
| **3 — AI-assisted review** | Human reviews AI output | Relativity aiR (privilege/review); Everlaw; Hebbia | Standard |

**The autonomy gap is wider than the marketing.** Harvey launched the **Legal Agent Benchmark (LAB)** on **May 6, 2026** — 1,200+ tasks, 24 practice areas, 75,000+ rubric criteria; top model (Opus 4.7) costs **~$50.90/task, ~22 min** (all confirmed). Grading is all-pass ("a deal memo that catches 8 of 10 material risks is not 80% useful"). **Correction per verdict:** the dossier attributed the *missing leaderboard* to "revealing how far agents are from autonomous practice" — that reasoning is a **third-party (LawNext) interpretation, not Harvey's stated reason.** Harvey's own stated reason is that it is partnering with Artificial Analysis to publish a *regularly-updated* leaderboard as new models arrive. **Foreground the benchmark's existence and the all-pass logic; drop the "withheld to hide weakness" framing.**

**Human-in-the-loop is hardwired by malpractice/regulatory pressure:** California Bar COPRAC approved proposed amendments **Mar 13, 2026** (45-day comment) requiring lawyers to independently verify all AI output; ABA guidance attaches the duty to the attorney personally, not the vendor; Relativity states "fully autonomous agents without human oversight do not belong in legal tech."

**What makes them say yes — three credible triggers:**

**Willingness-to-pay anchor — cost avoidance, not productivity ROI.** Comparables: AmLaw outside counsel bills $1,000+/hr; Harvey already clears $1,000–$2,000/seat/month. The #1 friction is therefore **budget reallocation, not price resistance.** Frame as *"one adverse sanctions motion costs more than 10 years of receipts,"* and code spend to an existing risk/compliance line.

**SKU dynamics:**
- **Displaces:** self-attested "audit log" features inside Harvey/Relativity/DISCO that are *not* court-admissible; Truescreen's manual per-document certification; traditional notary/apostille workflows.
- **Augments:** eDiscovery hosting contracts (adds a court-admissible mint tier); malpractice premiums (proves governance discipline); Harvey/Hebbia seats (adds an FRE-902 wrapper around in-platform agent actions).
- **Fastest route to close: ride a pre-approved vendor PO (Harvey/Relativity) as a module**, avoiding a standalone security review and DPA negotiation.

**Confidence caveat (verdict gaps):** The "lose preferred-risk standing within 18 months" claim cites *legalaigovernance.com/for-carriers/* — a **vendor-generated page, not a primary insurer/regulator source**; the specific 18-month window is **unverified against a named carrier.** Treat the insurer pull channel as a **strong exploratory hypothesis**, not an established fact, and validate it directly with a broker before building GTM around it. *US v. Heppner* (SDNY, oral ruling Feb 10, 2026; written Feb 17) is confirmed but **the holding turns on third-party disclosure breaking confidentiality and lack of attorney direction — not on "provenance" as a general requirement.** Do not paraphrase it as a provenance mandate.

## 6. Where Clockchain plugs in

**Layer priority (confirmed integration surface):**

1. **B5 Receipt Minting, shaped as an FRE 902(13)/(14) certification** — closes the self-authentication gap *today*. This is the wedge. It mirrors Page Vault's accepted qualified-person model, with the cryptographic notary replacing the vendor-CTO affidavit. **Frame it precisely: a *necessary but not sufficient* condition for admissibility** — it satisfies the self-authentication prong; opponents can still object on hearsay/relevance grounds. Never pitch it as a silver bullet.
2. **A4 Attestation (model version + delegation)** — the highest-value identity layer. **Harvey runs GPT, Claude, and Gemini concurrently, and its audit log does *not* capture model version as a discrete field** (confirmed from Harvey's developer docs). Hebbia's Matrix decomposes queries across multiple specialized agents. A4 answers the live, unanswered "*which model produced this clause extraction?*" question. Emit A4 at the MCP tool-call level; anchor via **B2 Identity Binding** before the response leaves the platform.
3. **B3 Timestamping with multi-source UTC** — differentiates from Truescreen's single-TSP eIDAS timestamp via BFT quorum (GPS + atomic + NTP). eIDAS Art. 42 gives qualified timestamps a legal presumption of accuracy and shifts the burden to the challenger — Clockchain matches that legal effect with cryptographic independence.
4. **A3 Delegation** — maps the law firm authorization chain (partner → associate → Harvey agent) to a cryptographic chain a court can inspect. This is the **malpractice shield** against the anticipated "agentic liability crisis," not just a compliance checkbox.
5. **B4 Subnet Anchoring** — satisfies FRCP 37(e) by freezing the receipt chain on a customer-dedicated subnet at hold-trigger time.

*A1/A2/A5/A6 (issuance, registry, revocation, verification) are supporting infrastructure — critical at scale, not the initial wedge.*

**Concrete integration shapes (in friction order):**

- **Primary — MCP server hook (B1 → B3 → B5).** Anthropic's May 12, 2026 "Claude for Legal" shipped 20+ MCP connectors (Relativity, iManage, NetDocuments, Everlaw, Ironclad, DocuSign). Clockchain's MCP server intercepts every tool-call result (document read, clause extraction, redline push) and fires **B1 Capture → B3 Timestamping → B5 Mint** before returning the result. Lowest friction: receipts attach to existing action calls, no new pipeline. Relativity Audit already attributes MCP actions to the authenticated OAuth user — so a Clockchain receipt produces **dual attribution: Relativity's mutable log + Clockchain's immutable B5.** *That duality is the sales argument: Relativity proves what happened inside Relativity; Clockchain proves it to a court without Relativity as a witness.*

- **Secondary — Harvey Audit Log API pull + notarized export.** Harvey exposes `GET /api/v1/logs/audit` (fields: UUID, action type, user, timestamp, IP; **60 req/min; 1-year retention; mutable server-side**). Clockchain polls/webhooks each event and re-anchors it (**B4**), producing a B5 receipt presentable under FRE 902(13)/(14) **without calling Harvey's engineering team as a witness.** Gap closed: Harvey's log is *stored by Harvey*, not an independent notarial act — and it has no model-version field (the A4 gap).

- **Tertiary — Relativity load-file (DAT+OPT) augmentation.** Add a column to the DAT (or a sidecar manifest) carrying the B5 receipt hash + ledger URL for each AI-touched document. Relativity's **May 21, 2026 Claude Compliance API** integration ingests Claude Enterprise conversation data (chats, uploads, projects) into RelativityOne — **a new first-party ESI category.** This is a forcing function: those Claude artifacts need B5 receipts *at creation time* (in Harvey/Claude) to be self-authenticating when they later land in Relativity for review.

- **Legal Hold Trigger Callback (FRCP 37(e)).** When Microsoft Purview or Logikcull issues a hold (webhook / Power Automate), **B4** freezes the relevant receipt chain on a customer-dedicated subnet — a tamper-evident preservation record meeting the "reasonable steps to preserve" standard. Current eDiscovery platforms do not auto-ingest AI agent logs as hold-subject ESI.

- **WORM connector.** B5 receipts written to the firm's existing S3 Object Lock / Azure Immutable Blob at mint time, with the Clockchain ledger as the authoritative provenance anchor — the SIEM/SOAR immutable-sink pattern (Kafka → S3 WORM) applied to receipt data, satisfying SEC 17a-4 / FINRA-equivalent retention.

## 7. Risks & open questions

**Down-weighted / corrected claims (do not carry forward as stated):**
1. **FINRA "must retain intermediate tool calls/decision pathways" is a law-firm gloss, not FINRA text.** Cite only as a directional signal.
2. **ABA Op. 512 "47 state bars adopted," "review must be logged," "reconstruct what the AI did"** — all refuted/unverifiable. Keep only: Rule 5.3 extends to AI; attorney review required.
3. **Harvey LAB leaderboard "withheld to hide weakness"** — that motive is third-party interpretation; Harvey plans a live leaderboard.
4. ***US v. Heppner* is about confidentiality/attorney direction, not a general provenance mandate.**

**Honest gaps (from the adversarial verdict):**
- **Rule 707 timeline is far from certain.** Beyond the May 7, 2026 committee vote, the rule still needs Judicial Conference endorsement, Supreme Court approval, and Congressional submission; the Dec 1, 2027 effective date is contingent (final report due ~June 2026). **Enterprise legal sales cycles may stall awaiting finalization.**
- **The "basic scientific instruments" exemption in Rule 707 is untested** — scope unknown.
- **eDiscovery market size is single-sourced.** $16.9B→$25.1B at 8.25% CAGR (Fortune Business Insights) conflicts with Grand View ($31.5B by 2030, 10.7% CAGR). Present as *one estimate among several.*
- **Insurer pull channel is vendor-sourced and unverified** (see §5). The 18-month preferred-risk-loss window has no named-carrier confirmation.
- **"88% of enterprises had agent security incidents / 33% had no audit trail"** — citation chain (VentureBeat → blog) too indirect to confirm; **do not rely on it.**
- **DISCO 300% metric is platform-level, not Q&A-specific.**
- **Truescreen funding** is a range ($2.67M–$3.11M); "~$3.1M" is the high end.

**Structural risks:**
- **No rule names "autonomous agent actions" as a distinct category.** Enforcement uncertainty cuts both ways — slows mandated demand, but opens first-mover standard-setting.
- **Market education in the EU:** Truescreen explicitly markets *against* blockchain (cost, environmental, legal-recognition arguments). Clockchain will have to win that argument head-on in EU deals.
- **Vendor pivot risk:** Straiker ($21M, audit-grade traceability) or Truescreen (already has API + MCP endpoint) could pivot toward agent-action receipts. The five-primitive moat is the durable defense; speed to the FRE-902 reference architecture is the perishable one.
- **NIST has not set a standard.** Its 2025 AI Agent Standards RFI explicitly lists agent audit/non-repudiation as an *open question* — no mandatory standard yet. (W3C Verifiable Credentials and the ARIA protocol, NIST docket 2025-0035, are the emerging interoperability formats to track for A3 delegation chains.)
